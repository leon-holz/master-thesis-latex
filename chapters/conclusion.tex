\chapter{Conclusion}
\label{chap:conclusion}

\section{Summary and Key Findings}

This thesis investigated whether the popularity of Reddit posts can be predicted using semantic text embeddings and structured metadata, and whether such predictions can inform a practical automated posting pipeline. The work examined this question through two complementary lenses: offline cross-validation experiments on historical data, and a live deployment experiment involving model-guided post selection on Reddit.

The offline experiments demonstrated that Reddit post reception is predictable to a moderate extent using publicly available features. The trained XGBoost regression models achieved Spearman correlations exceeding 0.20 across multiple subreddits and mean absolute errors around 7\% when predicting upvote ratio. Semantic embeddings derived from the Stella 400M transformer model contributed substantially to predictive performance, outperforming classical TF-IDF representations and demonstrating that contextual text understanding captures meaningful signal related to community response. Additional structured features—including temporal patterns, author history, domain characteristics, and subreddit-specific metadata—further improved model accuracy. These results indicate that historical Reddit data contains detectable patterns related to post reception, and that such patterns can be extracted using off-the-shelf machine learning tools and publicly available archival data.

However, the live experiment revealed a sharp divergence between offline validation performance and real-world deployment outcomes. Model-approved posts—selected from the top 10\% of predicted upvote ratios—did not outperform randomly selected posts. In fact, model-approved posts achieved lower mean upvote ratios (0.808 vs. 0.872), lower scores, and fewer comments than the random baseline. More critically, predictions showed a strong negative correlation with actual outcomes within the model-approved subset ($\rho = -0.596$, $p < 0.0001$), indicating that the model actively selected posts in the wrong order. This deployment failure cannot be attributed to noise or weak signal alone—it represents a systematic inversion of the learned ranking.

The live experiment also revealed an important asymmetry: while model-guided selection failed, content generation succeeded. Randomly selected generated posts achieved a mean upvote ratio of 0.872, substantially exceeding the historical training baseline of 0.729 calculated from organic user-submitted content. This demonstrates that language models can produce posts that communities do not systematically reject, even when those posts originate from unfamiliar accounts. The primary point of failure was not the generation of plausible content, but the model's inability to predict which generated posts would perform well.

These findings collectively demonstrate that the technical barrier to constructing such a pipeline is low—publicly available data, transformer embeddings, gradient boosting, and generative language models can be combined into a functional system with modest computational resources—but that offline predictive accuracy does not guarantee deployment effectiveness. The gap between historical prediction and live performance points to a fundamental distribution shift: models trained on human-authored posts embedded in natural community dynamics fail when applied to generated content from low-authenticity accounts. This distribution shift may arise from several sources, including spurious correlations in the training data that do not generalize to generated content, differences in how communities respond to posts from unfamiliar versus established users, or systematic differences between historical organic posts and model-generated submissions that the feature representation cannot capture.

The thesis also documented substantial moderation resistance during deployment. Four of nine targeted subreddits banned the posting account, and an additional 17 posts were individually removed across remaining communities. These moderation actions occurred despite the generated content being topically relevant and stylistically similar to organic discussions, suggesting that automated posting faces social and procedural barriers beyond content quality alone. Community members identified the account as suspicious based on posting patterns, lack of comment engagement, and behavioral signals rather than textual content, indicating that successful deployment requires more than optimizing individual post quality.

These findings should be interpreted in light of several important constraints. The analysis covers only 19 subreddits and retains a filtered subset of the original data biased toward observable, retained posts. The live experiment used a newly created account with minimal behavioral sophistication and deployed only text posts, while the feature representation focuses on textual content and basic metadata without capturing the full social and behavioral context of Reddit participation. The causes of the observed distribution shift between offline validation and deployment outcomes remain incompletely diagnosed. A detailed discussion of these limitations and their implications appears in \autoref{chap:discussion}.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future_work}

Several promising directions emerge from the findings and limitations of this thesis.

\textbf{Addressing distribution shift.} The most critical direction for future work is developing methods to close the gap between offline validation and deployment performance. This could involve training models on generated content with observed outcomes, incorporating domain adaptation techniques to account for differences between historical and generated posts, or developing features that explicitly capture differences in how communities respond to content from unfamiliar versus established accounts. Understanding and mitigating distribution shift is essential for any practical application of such systems.

\textbf{Incorporating behavioral and social context.} Future models could integrate richer signals beyond textual content and basic metadata. This includes author participation history, comment interaction patterns, recent subreddit activity, and temporal dynamics within communities. Such features may better capture the social context that influences post reception and could reduce the gap between isolated textual analysis and the full social process underlying community response.

\textbf{Subreddit-specific modeling.} Rather than applying a single embedding prompt and feature set across all communities, future work could adapt the encoding process to the explicit rules, norms, and stylistic expectations of individual subreddits. This might include subreddit-specific prompts for generating embeddings, community-specific feature engineering, or separate models trained for each target subreddit. Such specialization may improve the representation of community-specific fit and help distinguish between generally plausible posts and posts genuinely appropriate for particular communities.

\textbf{Broader subreddit coverage.} Expanding the analysis to a larger and more diverse set of subreddits would enable testing whether the observed patterns generalize across different topics, moderation regimes, and community structures. This would also make it possible to investigate whether certain types of communities are more or less susceptible to successful prediction and deployment, and whether the distribution shift observed in this thesis is consistent across different subreddit categories.

\textbf{Temporally adaptive modeling.} Since subreddit norms and moderation practices change over time, future work could investigate rolling training windows, temporally aware embeddings, or continual learning approaches designed to account for drift. This would address the limitation that models trained on historical data may learn outdated patterns that no longer apply in current community contexts.

\textbf{Behaviorally realistic deployment experiments.} The live experiment used minimal behavioral sophistication. Future work could examine the influence of account age, comment activity, posting rhythm, and subreddit-specific participation history on post reception. This would clarify to what extent deployment success depends on textual properties versus broader authenticity signals, and whether behavioral augmentation can overcome the prediction failures observed in this thesis.

\textbf{Alternative prediction targets and evaluation metrics.} While upvote ratio is a useful indicator of reception, future work could investigate alternative targets such as total score, comment count, removal probability, or downstream discussion quality. Multi-objective models that jointly predict multiple aspects of post performance may provide a more comprehensive understanding of community response and enable more flexible deployment strategies.

\textbf{Model interpretability and feature analysis.} A closer examination of which features drive prediction performance could reveal whether models rely primarily on topical relevance, stylistic similarity, emotional framing, or metadata-based shortcuts. This would improve understanding of what the models actually learn and help assess whether learned patterns reflect meaningful community preferences or artifacts of dataset composition and filtering.

\textbf{Defensive applications.} The techniques explored in this thesis could potentially be adapted for defensive purposes. Similar methods might be employed to detect strategically generated content, identify suspicious posting behavior, or flag accounts exhibiting patterns consistent with automated optimization. Investigating such safeguards would be an important step toward balancing methodological innovation with platform integrity concerns, particularly given the demonstration that content generation succeeds even when prediction fails.

\textbf{Cross-platform generalization.} Future work could examine whether the approaches and findings generalize to other online platforms beyond Reddit. Different platforms have different interaction mechanisms, moderation structures, and community norms, and testing the pipeline on platforms such as Twitter, Hacker News, or specialized forums would clarify how platform-specific the observed patterns and limitations are.

These directions collectively point toward both improving the technical effectiveness of such systems and better understanding their limitations, failure modes, and implications for platform integrity. The deployment failure documented in this thesis suggests that closing the gap between offline validation and real-world performance is a substantial challenge that requires more than incremental improvements to existing methods.
