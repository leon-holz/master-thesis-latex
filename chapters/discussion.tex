\chapter{Discussion}
\label{chap:discussion}

The results of this thesis show that Reddit post popularity is predictable to a meaningful extent using publicly available textual and metadata features. Across multiple experiments, semantic embeddings and additional structured features captured non-trivial signal related to post reception. This indicates that the barrier to building a popularity prediction pipeline is relatively low, especially when large-scale archival data and modern embedding models are available.

At the same time, the results also demonstrate clear limits to such prediction. Even the best-performing models explain only a limited portion of the variance in upvote ratio, which suggests that Reddit post success is shaped by many factors beyond the observed feature space. Popularity on Reddit is not determined solely by wording or semantic content, but also by timing, visibility, moderation, author reputation, existing community dynamics, and early interaction effects. The results should therefore not be interpreted as showing that post success can be reliably engineered from text alone. Rather, they show that some aspects of community reception are predictable, while a substantial share remains outside the scope of the present pipeline.

A central limitation of the study lies in the composition of the dataset. Although the dataset is large in terms of total post count, the analysis covers only 19 subreddits. This is a very small subset compared to Reddit as a whole, and the selected communities are not necessarily representative of the broader platform. As a result, conclusions regarding cross-community generalizability, including the distinction between left-, right-, and neutral-labelled subreddit groups, should be interpreted cautiously. In particular, many of the subreddits categorized as neutral are discussion-oriented communities with their own strong posting norms and moderation practices. Differences observed between political leaning groups may therefore partly reflect differences in subreddit structure, expected post format, or moderation intensity rather than political orientation alone.

Another important limitation concerns data availability and selection bias. The final dataset was produced through multiple filtering stages. First, posts that could not be accessed or enriched through the Reddit API were removed, which reduced the Pushshift-based dataset by 23.38\%. Second, the minimum engagement constraint removed a further 35.3\% of the remaining posts. Relative to the initial dataset, only 50.2\% of posts were retained for modeling. These filtering steps improved the reliability of the target variable by excluding posts with missing metadata or extremely weak interaction. However, they also changed the composition of the dataset in systematic ways. The retained posts are more likely to reflect content that remained visible, complied with moderation rules, or received at least some degree of community attention. Consequently, the trained models do not represent all attempted participation on Reddit, but rather a filtered subset of posts that survived both archival and engagement thresholds.

This issue is likely even more severe than the retained dataset alone suggests. Posts that were removed immediately by moderators, blocked by automated filters, or never successfully appeared in a subreddit may not be represented in the archival data at all. This means that the dataset may systematically exclude certain forms of disallowed, low-quality, or suspicious content before any filtering by this study begins. As a result, the training data is biased toward observable and retained posts, which limits conclusions about the full distribution of content users or automated systems might attempt to submit.

A further limitation is that the prediction target itself is imperfect. Upvote ratio is a useful operationalization of post reception, but it is not a pure measure of quality, relevance, or persuasive success. It reflects a mixture of textual quality, topical fit, community agreement, early vote dynamics, ranking effects, and platform-specific mechanisms. A post may achieve a favorable upvote ratio not only because it is well written or well targeted, but also because it was posted at a favorable time, encountering a sympathetic early audience. The model might therefore learn patterns associated with observed community response, but these patterns should not be confused with a causal account of what makes a post intrinsically good or persuasive.

The pipeline is also limited in its representation of social context. Reddit posts are rarely interpreted in isolation. Comment interactions, author history, continuity across prior submissions, and ongoing community-specific conversations often shape how a post is perceived. The present approach models each post primarily through its textual content and associated metadata, but it does not capture the interactive and relational dimension of participation in online communities. This becomes especially relevant in the live experiment, where generated posts may appear suspicious or low-authenticity not because of their isolated wording, but because they lack the broader behavioral patterns associated with genuine users. Community members explicitly pointed to this issue in responses such as ``This user has 58 OPs and 1 comment. Please don't expect any engagement.'' or ``Why does op constantly post non sequitor one lines every day? Are they training an LLM or just a bot themselves.'' These reactions suggest that users do not evaluate posts only based on textual content, but also based on visible account behavior and participation patterns.

This also highlights a broader deployment limitation: strong offline performance does not automatically translate into real-world effectiveness. Historical prediction models are trained on human-authored posts embedded in natural community dynamics. In contrast, the live experiment introduces generated content selected by the model itself, thereby changing the data-generating process. This constitutes a form of distribution shift. A model that identifies historically successful patterns may still fail when those patterns are reproduced by low-authenticity generated posts, especially if such posts violate implicit social expectations of the target community. The live experiment therefore provides an important counterbalance to the offline results by showing that model-guided selection alone is insufficient to guarantee strong real-world outcomes.

Temporal instability presents an additional limitation. Reddit communities evolve over time, and both user expectations and moderation rules may change substantially. Posting formats that were previously common and successful may later become discouraged, redirected into megathreads, or removed altogether. For example, a subreddit may historically reward a certain type of recommendation request while later deciding that such posts should only appear in designated discussion threads. Since the training data does not always encode these normative changes explicitly, models trained on historical data may partially learn outdated signals. This reduces the long-term stability of the approach and complicates deployment in changing online environments.

The live posting setup likely also underestimates what a more sophisticated malicious actor could achieve, while at the same time overestimating how transferable the present results are to broader misuse scenarios. On the one hand, the experiment used a relatively constrained setup and did not fully exploit tactics such as aged accounts, community-specific personas, richer comment interaction, or coordinated multi-account behavior. On the other hand, even if such tactics improved practical effectiveness, the present findings cannot simply be generalized to all subreddits or future platform conditions. The real risk therefore lies not in the exact performance achieved in this thesis, but in the demonstration that meaningful predictive signal can be extracted from publicly available data and integrated into an automated selection pipeline with comparatively modest resources.

This leads to an important ethical implication. A system of this kind could be misused not only to amplify misinformation, but more broadly to optimize manipulative participation in online communities. Potential abuse includes automated testing of content likely to receive favorable feedback, adaptation to community-specific norms in order to evade suspicion, and scaling of low-authenticity participation across multiple forums. Even if the present pipeline has clear limitations, the fact that it can predict aspects of community reception at all raises platform integrity concerns. In this sense, the main lesson of this thesis is not that Reddit popularity can be predicted perfectly, but that enough of the signal is accessible to make automated influence-oriented experimentation plausible.

Taken together, these findings indicate that semantic and structured features capture a meaningful part of Reddit post reception, but they do not represent the full social process underlying popularity. The observed predictive performance should therefore be understood as evidence of partial predictability under the specific conditions of this study, rather than as proof that post success can be universally optimized. At the same time, the limitations identified in this chapter point to several promising directions for future work, including the incorporation of comment context, richer behavioral histories, more diverse subreddit samples, and temporally adaptive modeling strategies.
