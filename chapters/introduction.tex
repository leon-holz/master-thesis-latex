\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chap:introduction}

This thesis investigates whether the reception of Reddit posts can be predicted and used to guide automated content generation and deployment, using only publicly available data and off-the-shelf machine learning tools. Beyond its practical applications, this question also carries adversarial implications: it asks how easily such a system could be constructed without specialized resources or privileged access. Reddit provides a natural testbed for this investigation, as a large-scale social media platform where user feedback directly shapes content visibility through upvotes and downvotes.

Popularity on social media platforms is not a monolithic concept. Absolute engagement measures such as raw score are shaped by exposure effects, platform-specific ranking mechanisms, and timing~\cite{hessel2019something, stoddard2015popularity}, which makes them difficult to compare across communities. This thesis therefore focuses on predicting how positively a post will be received by its community rather than how much attention it will attract. Popularity is operationalized as a normalized upvote ratio, framed as a continuous regression problem using only information available at post creation time. Compared with raw score or comment volume, this target more directly captures community approval and is less dependent on differences in exposure across subreddits.

Building on this formulation, the thesis investigates whether Transformer-based text embeddings combined with static metadata can predict post reception across diverse subreddits. In addition to offline evaluation on historical data, the work extends to a live deployment setting: posts generated by a large language model are scored by the trained popularity predictor and selectively published on Reddit. This live experiment serves a dual purpose. It provides an external validity check on the predictive model, and it demonstrates that the full pipeline - data collection, model training, content generation, automated posting - is operationally viable without specialized resources. Notably, the live experiment yielded a negative result: model-approved posts significantly underperformed randomly selected posts, with an observed performance difference of approximately 75\% lower upvote ratios. This finding is discussed in detail in \autoref{chap:results}.

The experiment was designed to limit real-world impact. Candidate posts were sampled from a fixed pool without targeting trending topics or exploiting current events, no adaptive feedback loop between prediction and observed outcomes was employed, and generated content reflects community-typical discussion prompts rather than coordinated messaging. These constraints are methodological choices, not inherent properties of the approach; a less restrained instantiation of the same pipeline would likely face relatively few additional technical barriers.

The contributions of this thesis are threefold:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the development of a popularity regression model that predicts relative community reception using only information available at post creation time,
    \item a systematic evaluation of Transformer-based text embeddings and auxiliary features for popularity prediction across politically diverse subreddits, and
    \item a proof-of-concept demonstration that publicly available data and widely accessible tools are sufficient to construct and deploy an automated, model-guided content pipeline on a major social media platform.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Ethical Motivation and Risk Assessment}

While the technical contributions of this thesis are framed as predictive modeling and system evaluation, the broader motivation is a warning. The demonstrated pipeline - combining publicly scraped Reddit data, off-the-shelf language models, and standard regression techniques - represents a reproducible blueprint for automated, model-guided influence operations on social media platforms.

The influence operations literature documents extensive real-world deployments of coordinated inauthentic behavior~\cite{woolley2018computational, linvill2020troll}. 
\autoref{sec:influence_ops} provides a detailed review of these campaigns and their implications for the present work.
What these documented cases often have in common is reliance on manual coordination, specialized infrastructure, or proprietary tools. The present work demonstrates that a functionally similar capability can be constructed using only publicly available data and standard open-source components.

The accessibility of this approach raises three interconnected concerns. First, the pipeline enables systematic A/B testing of generated content against predicted community reception, allowing rapid iteration toward well-received posts across thousands of candidates and dozens of target communities. Second, rather than deploying generic bot content, the regression model learns subreddit-specific norms directly from historical data, producing posts aligned with local discourse patterns that are substantially harder to detect than crude spam. Third, the resource requirements are modest: no privileged platform access is required, and the computational demands are well within the reach of cloud computing services or academic facilities.

The deliberate design constraints applied in this study - fixed candidate pools, no adaptive feedback loops, no exploitation of trending topics - were chosen specifically to limit real-world impact. However, these constraints are methodological choices rather than technical barriers. An adversary operating without such self-imposed limitations would face few additional obstacles to deploying a more aggressive variant of the same pipeline. The low barrier to entry is itself a finding, and one that warrants attention from platform operators, policymakers, and researchers concerned with online information integrity.

\subsubsection*{Thesis Structure}

The remainder of this thesis is structured as follows. \autoref{chap:basics} introduces the conceptual and technical foundations required for understanding Reddit engagement dynamics and the employed modeling techniques. \autoref{chap:relatedwork} reviews related work on popularity prediction and online engagement. \autoref{chap:methodology} describes the dataset, feature extraction, and modeling methodology. \autoref{chap:results} presents experimental results, followed by a discussion of limitations and implications in \autoref{chap:discussion}. \autoref{chap:conclusion} concludes the thesis and discusses possible directions for future work.
