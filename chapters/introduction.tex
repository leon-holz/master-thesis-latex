\chapter{Introduction}

Reddit is a large-scale social media platform where users engage in discussions 
across topic-specific communities known as subreddits. Within these communities, 
posts are evaluated through explicit feedback mechanisms such as upvotes and 
downvotes, which collectively influence content visibility and shape ongoing 
discourse. With hundreds of millions of active users and a substantial role in 
political discussion and news consumption, Reddit has become an important object 
of study for understanding online engagement and information dynamics~\cite{redditinc,pewresearch}.

The ability to anticipate how online content will be received has significant 
implications for marketing, recommendation systems, and the study of social media 
engagement~\cite{deaton2017using}. At the same time, the same predictive capability 
raises a less frequently examined question: how easily could such a system be 
assembled by an actor with adversarial intent, using only publicly available data 
and off-the-shelf tools?

This thesis addresses that question directly. A large-scale archive of Reddit 
posts is publicly accessible and trivially scrapable. \cite{baumgartner2020pushshift, redarcs} General-purpose language models capable of generating plausible discussion content are freely available. \cite{muennighoff2022mteb}
Gradient boosting models of the kind used in this work\cite{chen2016xgboost} require no specialized infrastructure. Taken together, these conditions suggest that assembling these components into a functional pipeline for automated, model-guided content deployment on social media is feasible without specialized infrastructure or privileged access — and that demonstrating this concretely is a contribution in itself.

Popularity on social media platforms is not a monolithic concept. Absolute engagement measures such as raw score are shaped by exposure effects, platform-specific ranking mechanisms, and timing\cite{hessel2019something, stoddard2015popularity}, which makes them difficult to compare across communities. This thesis therefore focuses on predicting how positively a post will be received by its community rather than how much attention it will attract. Popularity is operationalized as a normalized upvote ratio, framed as a continuous regression problem using only information available at post creation time. Compared with raw score or comment volume, this target more directly captures community approval and is less dependent on differences in exposure across subreddits.

Building on this formulation, the thesis investigates whether Transformer-based 
text embeddings combined with static metadata can predict post reception across 
diverse subreddits. In addition to offline evaluation on historical data, the 
work extends to a live deployment setting: posts generated by a large language 
model are scored by the trained popularity predictor and selectively published 
on Reddit. This live experiment serves a dual purpose. It provides an external 
validity check on the predictive model, and it demonstrates that the full 
pipeline — data collection, model training, content generation, automated 
posting — is operationally viable without specialized resources.

The experiment was designed to limit real-world impact. Candidate posts were 
sampled from a fixed pool without targeting trending topics or exploiting 
current events, no adaptive feedback loop between prediction and observed 
outcomes was employed, and generated content reflects community-typical 
discussion prompts rather than coordinated messaging. These constraints are 
methodological choices, not inherent properties of the approach; a less 
restrained instantiation of the same pipeline would likely face relatively few additional technical barriers.

The contributions of this thesis are threefold:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the development of a popularity regression model that predicts relative 
    community reception using only information available at post creation time,
    \item a systematic evaluation of Transformer-based text embeddings and 
    auxiliary features for popularity prediction across politically diverse 
    subreddits, and
    \item a proof-of-concept demonstration that publicly available data and 
    widely accessible tools are sufficient to construct and deploy an automated, 
    model-guided content pipeline on a major social media platform.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this thesis is structured as follows. 
\autoref{chap:basics} introduces the conceptual and technical foundations 
required for understanding Reddit engagement dynamics and the employed modeling 
techniques. Chapter~3 reviews related work on popularity prediction and online
